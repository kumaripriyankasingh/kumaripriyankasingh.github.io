# Just When I thought I figured out
We often spend a massive amount of time trying to build a predictable life. We set rules for ourselves, seek out a kind of inner "peace," and convince ourselves that we finally understand who we are and how the world works. It is easy to believe we have reached that quiet place where everything is settled, our minds are made up, and our opinions are solid.


But life rarely cares about our plans. It loves to let us feel comfortable for a moment, only to remind us that we are actually not in control at all. The ancient philosopher Heraclitus famously said, 
>"Everything flows." 

He believed you could never step into the same river twice, because the river is always changing, and so are you. It is a profound realization of how deeply true that is. We try to freeze the river, to build a house on moving water, and then we are shocked when we get swept away.


The Stoic thinker Epictetus taught that we suffer not from the events in our lives, but from our rigid expectations of them. Often, our core expectation is that life will just stay quiet. We work hard to master the art of solitude, making peace with our carefully constructed routines.

Then, out of nowhere, a profound shift arrives that changes everything. It isn't born of hard work; it just is.

It brings a depth of clarity and a natural comfort that feels entirely new. It is often the very reality we once wanted so badly, arriving precisely when we have completely stopped looking for it. It is a massive shock when reality actually delivers what was once desired. It is beautiful, but it is also a harsh reminder: we might be experiencing this moment, but we are not in charge of the story.


The philosopher Søren Kierkegaard wrote that 
> "Life can only be understood backwards; but it must be lived forwards." 

In the wake of unexpected beauty, we find ourselves stumbling forward in the dark, trying to make sense of a plot twist we didn't write.

This inevitably leads to a haunting question: If something is so good and feels so right, should it not stay?

We are usually taught that things end because they are broken or because of some huge conflict. But the deepest ache comes when a reality feels utterly seamless, yet you can feel the time running out. It defies logic. Seneca once wrote that everything we cherish is essentially on loan to us from the universe, and we should never be surprised or angry when it is called back. But knowing that intellectually does not stop the ache in the chest when the collection notice arrives.

It leaves you asking the silence: **Why are we shown this light, only for it to become a memory?**

Is life just being cruel? Or is there a reason for this harsh lesson? Perhaps these moments aren't sent to stay forever. Perhaps they are just sent as evidence. 
A way of life saying: “See? This depth exists. It is real. And you are capable of experiencing it. Now, can you let it go without falling apart?”

The poet and thinker Rainer Maria Rilke offered this advice for times like this:
  
>"Let everything happen to you: beauty and terror. Just keep going. No feeling is final."

These sudden shifts in life are exactly that—beauty and terror wrapped into one. Life is a rough teacher. It doesn't ask gently; it uses a sledgehammer to make sure the old ideas we held so tightly are completely smashed to make way for something new.

It is easy to be standing there completely surprised—not just by the world, but by our own arrogance in thinking we had it all figured out. Every time we think we have written the last chapter, a blank page appears with a hard question on it.

There is wisdom in looking at life like the Japanese art of Kintsugi, where broken pottery is repaired with gold. The piece is defined by its breaks, and it’s actually more beautiful for having been shattered. As Leonard Cohen famously sang, 
> "There is a crack in everything, that's how the light gets in."

If you find yourself today thinking you finally understand "the way things are," or that your path is set, take a deep breath. Look around. Life is probably preparing its next huge surprise for you. It is terrifying and beautiful all at once.

The only thing we can really do is stay open to being shattered...
